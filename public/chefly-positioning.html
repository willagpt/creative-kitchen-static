chefly = food without the project.

The category got crowded. Everyone's making the same claims. Here is what Chefly is, what Chefly is not, who fronts it, and how to test every single ad against it. One page. Printable. Read before you brief anyone, film anything, or ship any copy. Note: this doc covers the consumer (DTC) brand only. Chefly Trade (the wholesale arm) is fronted by James and has its own positioning one-pager.

the one-page version

What Chefly is
Food without the project. Proper food, by a cook who learnt because she got tired of what was out there, sent to people who'd rather not plan their week in a spreadsheet. Sous-vide proteins, the same restaurant technique we already supply to chefs, because protein is the bit most meal prep ruins.
Who fronts it
Kaja. 58. Self-taught. Has cooked every Chefly recipe since day one, seven years ago. Warm, confident, country-grounded, happy on camera.
Who supports
James. 43. In food since 2007. Runs the business. On camera as the customer-proxy ("husband at the table"), the founder-story voice, and the strategic commentator. Not the chef. Doesn't pretend to be. Also fronts Chefly Trade (wholesale) separately.
Together
20 years. Visible, not stated. Lived-in. The rhythm is a long partnership, not a brand-couple dynamic.
Enemy
Food as a self-improvement project. Macro counters. Meal plans. Wellness-industrial complex. "Fuel, not food." The whole optimisation pitch.
Line we own
"Dinner, handled." Plus up to four supporting. See sentences section.
Brand world
Warm. English country. Real kitchen. Yellow-and-olive palette. Home, not studio. A lived-in house, not a ghost kitchen.

section 1

the worldview

This is what Chefly believes. Every ad, email, post, packaging line, supplier call and creator brief traces back here.

Every DTC meal brand in the UK is selling food-as-a-project. Simmer sells a training project. Calo sells a macro project. Your Heights sells a biohacking project. Huel sells an efficiency project. Frive sells a performance project. Different costumes, same pitch: care more, plan more, track more, optimise more.

Chefly is the inversion of that entire category's offer.

Most people do not want food to be a homework assignment. They want dinner to be sorted. Chefly is for people who don't want food to be a project. No plan. No programme. No tracking. No transformation arc. Proper food. Handled.

This isn't a softer version of Simmer. It's a different emotional job. Simmer sells you a better version of yourself through food. Chefly sells you one less thing to think about. Bigger market, warmer relationship, longer retention.

And while everyone else fights over macros, programmes and points, we fixated on the bit that actually goes wrong: the protein. Every meal-prep app gives you 180g of chicken and assumes you'll cook it right. You won't. They know you won't. Restaurants stopped pretending decades ago and went sous-vide, the technique we already supply to the chefs cooking next to you on Friday night. Same fish, same chicken, same brisket, cooked precisely, finished fast. Protein is the meal. We treat it that way.

section 2

the enemy (name it out loud)

Opposition is clarifying. Brands that try to appeal to everyone appeal to no one. Chefly picks a fight with a specific culture.

The enemy is food-as-a-self-improvement-project. The wellness-industrial complex telling everyone their dinner has a job to do beyond feeding them. Meal plans. Macro trackers. "7-day resets." "30-day cuts." "Fuel, not food." Trainers in the kitchen. Nutritionists on the packet. The whole voice of "you are what you eat, so eat correctly." You don't attack competitors by name. You attack the category they represent. Every customer who's ever bought a meal from one of them and felt a little bit reduced to a macro calculation is your customer.

What the category sells

  • macros, protein, calories, fuel
  • what the body needs
  • the trainer / the nutritionist
  • the athlete / the biohacker
  • optimising your life
  • the transformation arc
  • the meal plan
  • "wellness"

What Chefly sells

  • taste, flavour, care, pleasure
  • what you actually want to eat
  • the cook (Kaja)
  • a normal person, feeding you
  • making dinner one less thing
  • stability, not transformation
  • this week's menu
  • proper food
section 3

origin story (true, short, unfakeable)

Every competitor in this set has a credentialed founding myth. Ours doesn't. Ours is better because it's the customer's own story reflected back.

The facts: James has worked in food since 2007 swap in the specific food-CV line here. Seven years ago, James and Kaja were tired of what was out there. They started Chefly together. Kaja became the cook on day one and has developed every recipe since. James runs the business. They have been together twenty years.

"I've worked in food since 2007. Seven years ago Kaja and I were tired of what was out there, so we started cooking our own answer. Kaja became the cook. I still run the rest. That's Chefly."

Short About-page version:

"After nearly 20 years in food, I bet a company on Kaja's cooking. Seven years later, she still cooks every recipe."

Kaja's side of the same sentence:

"Since day one, I've developed every Chefly recipe. Tasted every meal. Been there every production day. Over a million meals. One cook."

Why this origin works:

  • "Tired of what was out there" is the enemy named in the founding sentence. The positioning and the origin are the same idea.
  • James as the informed outsider. 19 years in food means the rejection of the optimisation playbook is informed, not naive. You've seen the inside. You walked out. You bet on Kaja instead.
  • Kaja as the home cook who stepped up. Same genre as Ina Garten, Deliciously Ella, Mob Kitchen. Self-taught → beloved. British food culture rewards this.
  • Seven years of receipts. Chefly has a seven-year operating track record. Most UK DTC meal competitors are younger than that. Longevity is a quiet trust asset.
  • Sous-vide proteins, already in restaurants. Chefly supplies sous-vide proteins to chefs. The same technique the best UK restaurants use on the bit that matters most. The consumer claim is underwritten by a working B2B kitchen. It's not a marketing line, it's a second revenue stream with its own customers.
section 4

voice lead · kaja

The brand's main character. Not the chef. The cook. 80% of camera time is hers.

Who she is, on camera: Kaja, 58, self-taught, English country kitchen. Warm. Confident. Happy to be on camera and up for trying. The cook who has developed every Chefly recipe since day one. Not a chef. Not a nutritionist. Not a trainer. Just a cook. The tone is warm aunt who feeds you, not fitness influencer.

What she's actually done (the receipts): Developed every Chefly recipe since day one. Tasted and approved every single meal. Present in the kitchen every production day. Over a million meals, to date. No team of chefs, no rotating R&D, no outsourced development. One cook. The whole time.

Why her, specifically:

  • There is no dominant female-founder-led voice in UK DTC food. Simmer, Huel, Gousto, Mindful Chef all skew male-voice primary. Calo has no voice. The slot is structurally open.
  • Age is credential, not liability. The UK food space rewards older voices: Mary Berry (90), Nigella (65), Delia (84), Prue Leith (85), Rick Stein (78), Ina Garten (77). Food trust compounds with age. The "young person's game" idea is a TikTok hangover; Meta ads skew 35-65.
  • The worldview needs her. "Don't count macros, just eat proper food" lands as lazy from a 28-year-old and as wisdom from a 58-year-old who has actually cooked a lot of dinners. The messenger is the message.
  • She fails the competitor swap test most cleanly. Simmer, Calo, Frive literally cannot make her ad.

What she does on camera (seven of the things she is best placed to front):

  1. Recipe reveal — "this week's dinners." Warm kitchen, her voice, her hands.
  2. Taste sessions — her tasting and signing off (or rejecting) a new dish.
  3. "Why I cook it this way." Short-form explainers that are not instructional, they're opinions.
  4. Home dinners — what she's actually eating tonight at her own kitchen table.
  5. Seasonal drops — she announces the new menu the way a restaurant unveils one.
  6. Direct to customer — voice notes, hello-from-the-kitchen clips, thank-yous.
  7. The manifesto piece — one to camera piece, two minutes, "why we don't do meal plans."
section 5

  • Speaks on record about "why we don't do meal plans"

Strategist & commercial operator

Runs marketing, growth, brand, supplier relationships, paid media, the business. Mostly off-camera. Named in credits, visible in business-side content.

  • Supplier story pieces (visits, handshakes)
  • "Why we chose this packaging" explainers
  • Annual "how Chefly made a difference" round-ups
Share of camera: ~5% · business-side only
role 3 of 4

Customer proxy (the husband at the table)

In couple content, James is the customer. He arrives, he eats, he reacts. Viewers see themselves in him. Kaja provides the answer his arrival sets up.

  • Kitchen-table couple format (ask / cook / taste / eat)
  • "What did you think?" taste reactions
  • Evening dinner at home footage, real, unscripted
Share of camera: ~15% · couple scenes
role 4 of 4

The long-form voice

Where explanation, commentary, and opinion are needed, James carries it. Longer formats. Podcast guests. Press interviews. Investor-deck narrative.

  • Press / podcast interviews on behalf of Chefly
  • Founder letter inside first-order packaging
  • Behind-the-scenes IG stories from the business side
Share of camera: ~5% · long-form only
The shape of the pairing She's the cook. He's the champion. The brand exists because he believed in her cooking for long enough to build a company around it. That sentence is the whole dynamic. Every couple shot either demonstrates that sentence or cuts it.
section 6

the couple dynamic

20 years together. 15-year age gap. This is the secret weapon. Don't verbalise it. Let the camera do the work.

What viewers will clock in half a second, and stop caring about in one: older partner, younger partner, long time together, lived-in kitchen, real home. That's the visual shorthand. It's warmer than Simmer's fit-couple dynamic. It's warmer than Frive's reluctant founder. It's warmer than any staged couple in the category because it isn't staged.

Rules for couple-format content:

  1. Don't say "wife" or "husband." Use Kaja's name. "Kaja's been cooking for me for years" reads warmer and names the main character.
  2. Don't say "20 years" in every piece. Once, on the About page, maybe in the founder long-form. It's ambient, not a headline.
  3. Don't say the age gap. Ever. Viewers clock it, that's enough.
  4. The rhythm is long-partnership, not brand-couple. Slightly bickering. Relaxed. Honest. The comedy of knowing each other for two decades. Reference feel: Ina Garten & Jeffrey at home, not Simmer training together.
  5. The kitchen-table scene is the unit of couple content. Kaja cooks. James arrives. One of them starts talking. It works because it's real.
"Kaja's been cooking for me for years. It got so good I built a company around it." That's the whole couple story. Say it once, well, then just film them at the table.
section 7

brand world

What every photo, set, palette and soundtrack is trying to feel like. If it doesn't feel like this, it's not Chefly.

Overall feel: A lived-in English country home, slightly golden-hour light, dog on the rug, garden out the window. Warm, not clinical. Generous, not precise. Real, not aspirational. The opposite of a fitness studio and the opposite of a white-boxed industrial kitchen.

section 8

language rules

Every piece of copy gets pulled through this list before it ships. Bans are hard bans. Owned words should appear on purpose, repeatedly, across channels.

banned (do not use)

chef trained Michelin macros calorie protein-packed fuel optimise biohack wellness plan programme reset cleanse project transform premium crafted artisan subscription box trade wholesale Chefly Trade

owned (use on purpose)

cook Kaja kitchen dinner proper food real food handled sorted sit down tonight this week's menu made at home warm generous proper eat tired of that nothing fancy seven years sous-vide rested finished restaurant-grade protein already in restaurants the best bit cooked right
Also No em dashes, no en dashes. Use commas, colons, full stops or arrows (→). Ranges as "2 to 3" not "2-3." Lowercase starts where appropriate for warmth. Short sentences. "Chefly" is not a chef brand. "Chefly" is a vibe. Don't overclaim. "Trade" and "wholesale" are banned on the consumer side because they belong to Chefly Trade, the separate B2B brand. Don't cross the lanes.
Why "sous-vide" is allowed but "chef" is not "Chef" is a title and a posture. "Sous-vide" is a technique and a fact. We use it because restaurants use it, and we already supply it to them. It's not a credential play, it's a description of how the protein got cooked. Use it plainly: "sous-vide chicken," "sous-vide salmon." Don't dress it up. "Protein" on its own is fine when paired with doing-it-right language ("cooked right," "rested," "finished"). "Protein-packed," "high-protein," and "macros" stay banned because they belong to the optimisation lane.

section 11

the swap test

The one diagnostic every piece of content should pass. Print this. Pin it above the edit suite.

For every ad, post, email or piece of packaging copy, ask:

"Could Simmer, Calo, Frive, Huel or Your Heights have made this ad and swapped their logo in?"

If yes → kill it. It's blending. It reinforces the category, not Chefly.

If no → the reason it's un-swappable is the angle Chefly owns. Ship it, and duplicate the reason.

Most ads in UK DTC meal delivery pass the swap test in both directions. That is why everything looks the same and everyone's CPA is grinding. Your job, from this doc forward, is to ensure Chefly ads fail the swap test.

section 12

what to do this week

Cheap, low-risk, high-signal moves that make the positioning real before anything else gets shot.

Do these in the next seven days

  1. Strip "chef" from all Kaja-facing copy. Website hero, About, Instagram bio, product pages, packaging. Replace with "cook" / "Kaja" / "the woman behind the kitchen."
  2. Add the origin sentence to the homepage, above the fold. "We got tired of what was out there. So we started cooking." One line, with Kaja's name visible.
  3. Post the two country / kitchen photos of Kaja on Instagram. Caption in her voice: "Hi, I'm Kaja. I'm the cook behind Chefly. Seven years ago we got tired of what was out there, so I started making it myself. Here's what I cook this week." Measure engagement against the last 90 days of lifestyle ads. This is the zero-cost test.
  4. Pick a hero sentence from section 10 and lock it. Suggest "Dinner, handled." Everywhere. For six weeks. No variation.
  5. Run the swap test on the last 10 ads. Flag every ad that passes (i.e. could have been a competitor's). Replace or rewrite before next launch.
  6. Brief the next shoot from this doc, not from the format brief alone. Every concept must fail the swap test and lead with Kaja or the couple dynamic.
