C2 · James founder talking head

Two cameras rolling, one honest founder monologue. A longer-form piece where James tells his own story in his own voice, with B-cam coverage ready to cut to. This pack walks the dual-camera setup, clap-sync, lighting, and the 42-second script beat by beat.

Priority 2 Dialogue-led 2 cameras · simultaneous Tripod A + clamp B 30 to 45s final ~60 min on set Difficulty: medium

Learning goals

  • Set two iPhones rolling simultaneously and keep them in sync with a clap
  • Position a 3/4 B-cam so the editor has a genuinely different angle to cut to
  • Light a talking head with a top-key softbox so it reads like window light, not a studio
  • Route DJI Mic 2 to a single camera on purpose (A-cam) and plan around B-cam having no mic
  • Coach James out of "founder voice" and into "coffee chat" voice

Pre-requisites

  • C1 complete and wrapped (same audio kit is re-used here)
  • Read Training Companion Parts 1, 2, 3 and Part 5 §C2
  • Both iPhones charged above 80%. Both tripods/clamps physically on the set.
  • James has rehearsed the 42s script aloud once, not memorised

What you'll deliver

  • 3 clean A-cam takes of the 42s monologue (tripod, dead-on eye level, mic'd)
  • 3 clean B-cam takes of the same script (clamp, 3/4 to James's right, silent)
  • 4 silent B-roll beats: laptop + container, hands on the box, microwave beep, eating at desk
  • A clap at the head of every simultaneous take so the editor can sync A and B in one click

01 · Concept

What we're making and why it works

The founder story carries this ad. James on camera, talking plainly. The two-camera rig is in service of the edit, not the look.

C2 is the founder talking head: 30 to 45 seconds of James speaking straight to camera about why Chefly exists and why he eats the same thing himself. It is the longest monologue we shoot all day and the one that carries the most weight on trust.

Two cameras run at the same time. A-cam is the spine: an iPhone 15 Pro Max on a tripod, dead-on eye level, microphone on James. B-cam is the cut: an iPhone 17 Pro Max clamped off to James's right shoulder at a 3/4 angle, wider, silent. Both record the whole take. One clap at the top of every take lets the editor snap them together in a single click.

The reason we shoot two cameras for this one and only this one is the length. A 42-second monologue is too long to sit on a single angle. The editor needs a second angle to cut to, and that angle has to exist in the same take (not a second take) for the line to land.

Plain-english summaryJames sits at his desk, two phones rolling, DJI mic on him. He claps once at the start, tells his 42-second story, holds the last look for 2 seconds, and stops both phones. Do that three times. Done.

Reference winners this pattern came from

  • Your Heights ad 2 — founder monologue, desk setup, two-angle cut. Scored well for founder trust.
  • Frive founder clip — same two-cam treatment, A-cam dead-on + B-cam 3/4. Their cut at 0:14 is exactly the move we're setting up for.
  • Both ads keep the camera still on both angles. No zoom, no push-in. The movement is in the edit, not the lens.

04 · Lighting

Lighting setup, step by step

Top key + small fill. Two lights, both daylight. Treat the Amaran as an overhead skylight.

Top-down plan · two cams, two lights
Amaran overhead as top key. Lantern 26" camera-left as soft fill. A-cam on tripod dead-on. B-cam clamped at James's 4 o'clock.

Step by step

  1. Place James in his chair first. Desk in front of him, laptop open, Chefly container to his left. Get him comfortable before you touch a single light.
  2. Boom the Amaran 200x overhead. Roughly 30cm in front of James's forehead and 30cm above his head. Pointed down and slightly forward at 45°.
  3. Fit the Dome III softbox with its front diffusion grid. The broad face must point down at James.
  4. Set the Amaran to 5600K (daylight) and 25 to 35% brightness. Look for soft light across James's forehead with just enough shadow under his nose to add shape.
  5. Check for raccoon eyes. If James's eye sockets are dark pits, the light is too steep. Lower the Amaran stand by 10cm or step it forward 20cm and re-check.
  6. Add the Lantern 26" as fill. Camera-left, roughly at James's shoulder height, 2m from him. Daylight 5600K, 10 to 15%. It should soften the under-chin shadow and add a second catch-light in his eyes.
  7. Kill the overhead kitchen/office lights. Tungsten ceiling lights will colour-clash with the 5600K rig and tint James orange on camera.
  8. Walk a loop around James with your phone in camera preview. Both on A-cam eye-line and B-cam 4 o'clock. Confirm no hot-spot on the forehead, no nose shadow across the lip, and catch-lights in both eyes.
Rule of thumbTop key = shape. Fill = softness. If the shape is dramatic it's "interrogation". If there's no shape at all it's "passport photo". Aim in between.
05 · Audio + clap sync

Audio setup and clap-sync procedure

Mic goes to A-cam only, on purpose. The clap is what makes the two-camera edit possible.

Audio routing

  1. DJI Mic 2 RX into A-cam (iPhone 15) USB-C. Not B-cam. Not "both, for safety". One route, one source of truth.
  2. TX on James. Clip under the collar, cable tucked, transmitter in his back pocket.
  3. Confirm on A-cam: open camera, start a dummy recording, speak, stop, play back. You should hear James clearly through the DJI. If you hear the iPhone onboard mic instead, stop and fix the input source in iPhone Settings before rolling.
  4. B-cam records onboard audio. That audio is throwaway. It exists only as a back-up scratch track in case the DJI fails mid-take.

Clap sync

  1. At the head of every take: Kaja taps record on A-cam first, then B-cam, then steps back into frame.
  2. Kaja stands between the two cameras, in a spot where she is visible to both, and claps once sharply. Not polite. Not soft. One real clap.
  3. Kaja says "rolling, clap, take one" out loud, claps, steps out of frame.
  4. James waits 2 seconds, then begins the monologue.
  5. At the end of the take, James holds the last look for a 2-second beat. Kaja stops B-cam first, A-cam second (mirror of start).
Why the clap mattersThe editor drags both clips into CapCut, finds the clap spike on A-cam's waveform, finds the visual clap on B-cam, aligns them, and the two angles are synced to the millisecond. No clap? You'll spend 20 minutes nudging B-cam back and forth and it'll still drift over 42 seconds.
06 · Wardrobe and set

Wardrobe and set dressing

James looks like a working founder, not a model. Desk looks like a desk someone actually works at.

Do
  • A plain T-shirt or casual shirt, stone, navy, or charcoal
  • Hair the way you'd wear it to a team meeting
  • Laptop open with a real spreadsheet, email, or Asana tab loaded
  • A single Chefly container on the desk, lid off, fork beside it
  • A mug of coffee (half-full) to the right of the laptop
  • One small plant or a book behind you for depth
Don't
  • A logo tee. Any logo, including Chefly. It dates the video immediately.
  • Busy patterns or stripes. They alias badly on camera and pull focus.
  • A closed laptop or a staged-empty desk. Reads fake instantly.
  • Over-styled food. No garnish. No perfect fork angle.
  • A cluttered background with 6+ objects. Messy reads chaotic, not real.
  • Loud backdrop art. Anything eye-catching behind James will steal the shot.
07 · Script

The 42s script, annotated

James, say this once to rehearse. Do not memorise. The goal is coffee-chat voice, not podcast host voice.

0:00
"I started Chefly because I was sick of thinking about food." Open with a full stop, not a rising intonation. You are stating a fact, not pitching.
0:04
"I run a business. I've got two kids. I don't have the bandwidth to plan three meals a day." Count it on your fingers in your head. Small, real. If your hand moves slightly at "two kids" it lands better on B-cam.
0:10
"And every week it was the same loop: what do I cook, have I got the stuff in, is it actually healthy." Three beats, slight pickup in pace. Don't sigh at the end even if you want to. Let the line carry the frustration.
0:17
"So we built Chefly for people like me." Slow down. Eye contact. This is the hinge of the whole script.
0:20
"Twelve meals in a box, built with real ingredients, portioned so the macros actually make sense." Hand gesture allowed here. One small tap on the container. Don't lift it to the camera.
0:26
"You heat it up in 90 seconds, you eat it at your desk, you go back to doing what you actually care about." Three "you" beats. Keep them even. Glance at the laptop on "at your desk" if it feels natural. Don't force it.
0:33
"I eat this same box every week. Not because I have to. Because I don't want to think about dinner anymore." Land on "dinner anymore". Small smile allowed but only after the word lands.
0:40
"You're my exact customer. Welcome." The sign-off. Hold the look for a full two seconds after "Welcome" before anyone moves. This is the frame the editor will end on.
0:42
Text overlay in post: "Chefly. Built for busy. Chosen by the founder." Added in CapCut. Syne Extra Bold. Cream on near-black end card. Logo bottom-right.

10 · Quality gates

You're done when...

Do not wrap until all of these are ticked.

  • 3 complete A-cam takes of the full 42s script, eye-level, dead-on, locked tripod
  • 3 matching B-cam takes of the same three takes, 3/4 right, locked clamp
  • Every take has an audible clap at the head, visible on A-cam waveform

11 · Common mistakes + rescues

What usually goes wrong and how to fix it fast

B-cam and A-cam won't sync in the edit

Editor can't find the sync point. The clap was too soft, or the cameras were rolling long before the clap so the waveform peak is ambiguous.

RescueGo back, re-roll the takes with a louder, single, sharp clap right at the top after both cameras are rolling. One clap only. Don't "test-clap" beforehand or the editor will align to the wrong one.
James sounds like he's reading

Voice lifts into "pitch mode". Pace is even and deliberate. Sentences all end on the same note.

RescueKaja says: "tell me about it the way you'd tell your co-founder over a pint. I'm not writing it down." Immediate re-take. First take after a pattern-break is almost always the keeper.
Overhead Amaran casts a nose-shadow across his lip

Classic problem with top-keys too steep. Shadow of the nose lands on the upper lip and the delivery looks sinister.

RescueEither lower the Amaran by 15cm so the angle becomes more frontal, or step it forward 20cm so it's less directly overhead. Re-check in A-cam preview.
B-cam loses focus mid-take

Autofocus hunts when James shifts forward or back in the chair.

RescueBefore rolling B-cam, tap-and-hold on James's face in the camera preview until AE/AF LOCK appears. Focus is now pinned. Do this on both cams, every take.
The laptop screen reflects in James's glasses (if worn)

Very distracting on A-cam. Editor can't save it.

RescueClose the laptop halfway or dim the screen to 20%. Alternatively, tilt the laptop screen forward 10° so its glare bounces down, not toward the camera.
The sign-off line falls flat in every take

"You're my exact customer. Welcome." gets read too formal. Or too smiley. Either kills it.

RescueHave James say the line to Kaja (not the camera) as if she'd just walked up to his desk. Shoot that delivery. Then have him repeat it once to the lens. The second take will carry the warmth of the first.

13 · Self-check

Quick self-check

Five questions. Try them before opening the answers.

Before you shoot, can you answer these?
Why does the DJI Mic 2 RX plug into A-cam only, not both cameras?
Show answer
Answer: One route, one source of truth. If you split the mic across both, you introduce two audio tracks that can drift and you risk one of them being lower quality. Onboard audio on B-cam is a scratch back-up; the edit uses only A-cam's DJI track.
Kaja claps before both cameras are recording. What's the consequence?
Show answer
Answer: The clap ends up on only one camera's audio/video, or on neither. The editor has no common sync point and will have to nudge clips by eye across 42 seconds — which always drifts. Always roll A-cam first, then B-cam, then clap.
Why do we lock white balance on B-cam instead of leaving it on Auto?
Show answer
Answer: Auto white balance drifts as James moves or as sunlight through a window shifts. When the editor cuts from A-cam to B-cam mid-sentence, James's skin tone changes and it looks like a different shot. Lock WB to match A-cam (5600K) and the cut is invisible.
On which two lines is the editor most likely to cut from A-cam to B-cam, and why?
Show answer
Answer: 0:17 ("So we built Chefly for people like me") and 0:33 ("I eat this same box every week"). Both are hinge beats — the script turns here. An angle change on a hinge makes the turn feel more intentional.
If the DJI Mic 2 fails mid-take and you don't realise until playback, what's your rescue?
Show answer
Answer: Pull the onboard audio from B-cam and use it as the master audio. Quality drops but the take is usable. Sync it to A-cam's clap. This is why B-cam rolls onboard audio even though we "throw it away" on purpose.
