Creative Playbook

Cross-brand pattern library, brand playbooks, and production shot lists built from 6 top-performing competitor video ads (47 to 68 days active).

Cross-Brand Patterns

Recurring structures, hooks, and emotional levers found across all 6 top performers. These are what's working in the UK meal prep category right now.

Hook Frameworks

Hook

UGC Testimonial Open

Creator speaks directly to camera with a personal recommendation or habit statement. No brand mention in the first 2 seconds. Builds trust before selling.

Used by: Frive (Sophie), Simmer Days active: 50, 68
Hook

Problem Agitate

Opens with the audience's pain point (training demands, time poverty, bad food quality) then positions the product as the obvious fix. Works best with a credible messenger.

Used by: _heidilong (athlete) Days active: 47
Hook

Bold Statistic / Claim

"50% of the average diet is ultra-processed." Leading with a surprising data point that disrupts scrolling. Works especially well when the viewer already cares about the topic.

Used by: _heidilong (UPF ad) Days active: 51
Hook

Curiosity Gap / Interview

"What's one hill you'll absolutely die on when it comes to food?" Poses a question the viewer must stay to hear answered. Podcast/interview aesthetic lends authenticity.

Used by: Frive (founder) Days active: 51
Hook

Before/After Transformation

Opens with a specific, measurable result ("gained 7kg in 8 months"). The number gives credibility. The journey gives the viewer something to aspire to.

Used by: Scott Thomas Days active: 51

Narrative Structures

Structure

Credential → Proof → Offer

Establish the speaker's authority (athlete, nutritionist, founder), demonstrate the product through B-roll, then close with a percentage-off offer. The dominant structure across all 6 ads. Every single one follows this arc.

Frequency: 6/6 ads
Structure

Category Contrast

Position the product against an inferior alternative (supermarket ready meals, UPF-laden food). Visual contrast (split screen, supermarket aisle shots) makes the gap visceral. Used in 3 of 6 ads.

Frequency: 3/6 ads Brands: Frive, _heidilong
Structure

Benefit Stacking

Rapid-fire claim layering: "nutritionist approved, macro balanced, 100% natural, high protein." Usually placed mid-video as a text overlay burst. Present in 5 of 6 ads.

Frequency: 5/6 ads

Emotional Levers

Emotion

Trust (6/6)

Every ad leads with a trust mechanism: nutritionist credentials, Olympic athlete endorsement, founder transparency, specific transformation stats. Trust is the table-stakes emotion in this category.

Emotion

Aspiration (6/6)

Every ad sells a version of the viewer's future self: fitter, healthier, more disciplined. The product is framed as a tool for becoming, not just eating.

Emotion

Convenience (5/6)

Time-saving is the second most common sell. "7 hours a week saved," microwave shots, no-thinking-required messaging. Appeals to the busy professional who wants results without kitchen time.

Emotion

Health Anxiety / Fear of Compromise (3/6)

UPF messaging, seed oil avoidance, "no better than supermarket ready meals" framing. This is category-specific fear that works well with the health-conscious audience who reads labels.

Production Observations

Lo-Fi Wins

All 6 ads use UGC or UGC-style production. No polished studio work. Phone cameras, natural lighting, real locations (gyms, kitchens, offices, waterfront). The "roughness" is the feature, not the bug.

Full-Frame Dominance

85 of 86 total shots are full-frame. Split screens appear in only 2 ads (5 shots total). These top performers keep it simple: one subject, one frame, no complexity.

Medium Pacing is the Sweet Spot

5 of 6 ads use medium pacing (roughly 1.5 to 2.5 seconds per shot). Only 1 ad (Frive founder) runs slow. Fast-paced editing is absent from the top performers entirely.

Duration: 12 to 37 Seconds

Wide range, but the two highest-activity ads (Simmer at 68 days, Scott Thomas at 51 days) sit at opposite ends. Length matters less than hook quality and narrative tightness.

CTA Pattern: Offer-Led Close

5 of 6 ads end with a percentage-off offer (40% or 50% off first order/week). The discount is always the final frame. Only the Frive founder ad skips the in-video discount entirely.

Key Weakness to Exploit

Brand name mispronunciation appears in 3 of 4 Frive ads ("Thrive" instead of "Frive"). This is a real vulnerability: Chefly's name is phonetically unambiguous. Lean into it.

Competitive Gap Map

GapWhat Competitors DoChefly Opportunity
Female audience Only 1 of 6 ads features a female protagonist. Messaging skews heavily male, gym, bulking. Female-led content is massively underserved. Lifestyle, convenience, and wellness angles for women could differentiate immediately.
Food visually Every ad shows the container, not the food. No appetite appeal, no steam, no close-ups of ingredients. High-quality food B-roll would stand out. "This is what's actually inside" as a content angle.
Brand pronunciation Frive's name is mispronounced as "Thrive" in 3 of 4 ads. Chefly is unambiguous. Lean into name recognition and repetition.
Variety / menu No ad shows more than 2 to 3 different meals. Range is never the selling point. If Chefly has a larger or more interesting menu, "choice overload" content could work: fridge pulls, weekly rotations, "what I ate this week."
Professional production All 6 ads are lo-fi UGC. No one in the category is investing in polished video. A production company shoot would be genuinely novel. One high-production hero video could anchor the brand above the UGC noise.
Taste / enjoyment Zero ads focus on taste. It's all macros, protein, and health claims. "Actually delicious" positioning. Reaction shots, taste tests, flavour descriptions. Emotional rather than rational.
