C4 · Offer-led static frame

Three carefully composed still photographs and one three-second push-in. No talking, no motion, no stylists on set. Just the product, the offer text, and light that makes the container look genuinely appetising. This is the lowest-variance format we run, and it is the highest-ROI one when it wins.

Priority 4 Stills + 3s push-in 1 setup · fixed ProRAW 3 variants + 1 video ~45 min on set Difficulty: low to medium

Learning goals

  • Set up a locked-off tripod frame that will not drift between the stills and the push-in
  • Light a packaged food container with a softbox pair and a flag so the sleeve reads on camera
  • Use ProRAW for the stills and 4K 30fps for the push-in, understanding why each setting matters
  • Style the container, the backdrop, and the foreground props so the hero reads in 0.3 seconds
  • Shoot three colour-consistent variants that the paid-media editor can test against each other

Pre-requisites

  • C1 to C3 wrapped. The microphone kit is packed away. You need a quiet, still environment.
  • Read Training Companion Parts 2 (light), 3 (camera), and Part 5 §C4
  • iPhone 17 Pro Max charged past 40% and set to ProRAW + 48MP for the stills
  • Kaja has the container prepared, plated, and garnished before the camera goes on

What you'll deliver

  • 3 ProRAW stills at 48MP: straight-on hero, three-quarter angled hero, tight close-up on the food
  • 1 three-second push-in video (4K 30fps, locked start and end) for the performance-media cutdown
  • A colour-locked reference still (shot first) that every subsequent frame matches for white balance
  • All four assets delivered in the shared Drive folder, labelled and tagged, within 24 hours

01 · Concept

What we're making and why it works

Three still frames plus a gentle 3s push-in. This is the offer ad that runs under paid media.

C4 is the offer-led static frame: the format that lets the performance-media team test a headline, a price, and a product shot against each other without needing a person on camera. The creative has to carry all the emotional load through lighting, composition, and styling. When it works, it is the cheapest format to produce and the easiest to iterate.

We are shooting one locked tripod angle with one light recipe and two small changes. The camera never moves during the stills. Between frames Kaja rotates the container or swaps a prop, and we fire the shutter again. For the video we press record, wait a beat, Kaja steps out of frame, and the camera pushes in three seconds on a second tripod tap-focus. That is it. No zoom, no pan, no walking.

Plain-english summaryTripod. Container on a board. Two softboxes. Kaja styles the food. James presses the shutter three times for stills, then records one short push-in video. Job done in 45 minutes.

Reference winners this pattern came from

  • Huel £20 off ad — single locked frame, two colour variants, offer text overlaid in post. Ran for 9 months.
  • Allplants hero ad — three-quarter angled container on a linen napkin, warm key left, cool fill right. 4.2x ROAS.
  • Both ads succeeded because the product was the subject and the offer was the message. There was nothing to argue with.

03 · Framing

Framing, camera settings, and ProRAW

One locked frame, three micro-adjustments, zero handheld.

The camera goes on the tripod at desk height, looking down at roughly 25 to 30 degrees. The container sits centre-frame. There is breathing room above for a headline overlay and below for the offer text. Left and right margins are loose enough that we can later crop to 1:1 or 4:5 without losing the hero.

The key to C4 is ProRAW. This is Apple's 48MP uncompressed format. It captures ~50x more colour data than the standard HEIC JPEG. This matters because the performance-media editor is going to push the colour, pull the shadows, and overlay bright offer text. JPEG will fall apart. ProRAW will not.

Camera settings:

  • Mode: Photo → ProRAW MAX (48MP)
  • Lens: 1x (main sensor, not the telephoto)
  • Focus: tap the hot spot on the container lid and lock with long-press (AE/AF lock)
  • Exposure: pull the sun slider down one stop from auto — underexpose slightly, recover in RAW
  • Aspect ratio: 4:3 (widest native, crop later)
  • Grid: ON (rule of thirds)
  • Live Photo: OFF

Video settings (for the 3s push-in only):

  • Mode: Video → 4K at 30fps
  • HDR: OFF (the editor does not want to fight Dolby Vision)
  • Stabilisation: OFF (we are locked on a tripod, it will add digital drift)
Straight-on hero
Centre-frame container. Headline breathing room above. Offer zone below.
3/4 angled hero
Same height, container rotated 25°. Shows depth and the sleeve edge.
Close-up on food
Container fills frame. Texture, garnish, and grain read hero.

Tripod discipline. Once the tripod is locked, do not touch it. If you need to rotate the container, rotate the container. If you need to change focus, tap-focus on the screen. If the tripod moves between frames, the colour correction will not match between stills, and the editor will be unable to use them as a consistent set.

04 · Lighting

Lighting setup, step by step

Two softboxes, one flag. Key at camera-right, fill at camera-left, flag blocking spill. 5600K across both.

Static food advertising lives or dies on light quality. The camera does not forgive harsh shadows or mixed colour temperatures. We are running a soft key-and-fill recipe that gives the container a clear directional light, a shadow side that still reads detail, and a clean backdrop with no hot-spot falloff.

  1. Mount the Amaran 200x on the C-stand at camera-right, angled in at 45 degrees, and about 80cm from the container. Slide the Lantern 26" over it. Leave the skirt on. Dial to 70%.
  2. Set up the Amaran Dome III on a mini stand at camera-left, angled in at 30 degrees, about 60cm from the container. Dial to 30 to 40%. This is the fill, it should not compete with the key.
  3. Clip the black A4 card to a magic arm between the Dome III and the camera. Position it so it blocks the fill from hitting the front face of the container. You want the front face lit by the key only.
  4. Check the white balance. Both lights must be at 5600K. If one drifts to 5000K the container sleeve colour will shift across the three stills and the editor will have to re-grade each separately.
  5. Light-meter with the iPhone. Tap on the container lid. Pull the sun slider down one stop. The container should read slightly underexposed on the phone screen. In ProRAW that pulls back beautifully in post.
  6. Look for hot spots on the backdrop. If the linen has a brighter patch behind the container, nudge the key light 10cm higher or flag it with a second card.
Lighting plan (top-down)
Key camera-right, fill + flag camera-left, clean ivory backdrop.
Lighting plan (side view)
Key elevated at 45°, fill lower at 30°, flag between fill and front of container.
Do
  • Run both lights at 5600K — daylight-matched
  • Keep the key noticeably brighter than the fill (at least 2:1)
  • Under-expose one stop and recover in RAW
  • Shoot the colour checker first, then pull it out
Don't
  • Mix colour temps — don't use a tungsten fill
  • Let fill catch the front face of the container
  • Turn on the room overheads — they will pollute the backdrop
  • Bump exposure in ProCamera once locked
05 · Styling

Food styling and set dressing

If the food doesn't look good, the light won't save you.

This format lives entirely on how the container reads in the frame. Kaja handles all of the styling before James turns on the camera, and the camera does not go on the tripod until the food is plated. That sequence matters: if we set lights first and style second, we invariably re-light, which eats 20 minutes.

Plating rules for C4

  • Pick the meal that holds its shape. Curries are a disaster. Rice bowls and pasta work. The Mediterranean-style dishes photograph best.
  • Rotate the sleeve so the logo reads. The Chefly wordmark should be visible but not centred. Usually one-third from the left edge.
  • Add a garnish on top of the food itself: a sprig of parsley, a crack of pepper, a lemon wedge on the side. This gives the frame a colour pop and a texture focal point.
  • Leave one prop in the frame: a linen napkin folded loosely to the left, or a fork resting on the board. Two props and the frame feels staged. None and it feels empty.
  • Wipe the sleeve edge. Any fingerprint or sauce smear will multiply at 48MP.

What is on the table

  • The oak chopping board (long edge parallel to camera)
  • The heated Chefly container with food plated in it
  • One linen napkin, folded loose, to camera-left
  • One garnish element on the food
  • Nothing else. No water glass, no bread, no kitchen clutter.
06 · Wardrobe

Wardrobe (props only · no people)

No one is in shot. But the board, sleeve, and napkin are.

C4 has no person in frame, so "wardrobe" is really prop wardrobe. The three items in shot are the container, the board, and the napkin. Every other format uses natural textures, so these have to feel of a piece with them.

  • Container — Chefly branded sleeve, unblemished. If the sleeve is creased, swap it.
  • Board — warm oak chopping board. No cutting marks visible in frame. Lightly oiled with unscented mineral oil before the shoot so it reads rich on camera.
  • Napkin — unbleached linen, ivory or oatmeal. Never white-white, never patterned. Slightly rumpled, not folded crisp.
  • Garnish — green (parsley, thyme, rocket) or citrus (lemon wedge). Never red (visual clash with the accent orange).
Why props matter for a no-face formatEvery visual decision has to carry the emotional load. A clean, warm oak board says "I care about what I eat". A creased sleeve says "I microwaved this at my desk". You are directing a story with three objects.
07 · The three stills

The three stills, briefed

There is no dialogue to write. The brief is visual: the what, the why, and the composition rule.

Below are the three stills you will deliver. Shoot them in this order — the straight-on frame first because it is the fastest to match in post if anything drifts. Each takes 5 to 10 minutes and the light setup should not change between them.

Still 1
Straight-on hero — container centre-frame, camera at 25° top-down angle. Headline space above, offer space below. Shoot 3 frames, 1 second apart. Best one wins.This is the one the performance-media team will use 80% of the time. Nail this and the day is won.
Still 2
Three-quarter angled hero — rotate the container 25 to 30 degrees clockwise. Same camera, same lights, same exposure. Garnish now visible on the side. Shoot 3 frames.Do not move the camera. Only rotate the container. This keeps the frames colour-consistent.
Still 3
Close-up on food — push the tripod in, or swap to 2x lens. Container fills frame, food texture and garnish dominate. The sleeve is a small frame element, not the hero. Shoot 3 frames, tap-focus on the garnish.This is the texture shot the editor uses in carousels. The sleeve should not read in this frame — only the food.
Video
3-second push-in — return to the straight-on frame. Record for 4 seconds (1s buffer start and end). Very gentle forward tripod push or zoom crop in edit. No handheld. No pan. No camera move.If you can't push in physically, shoot it static. The editor will add a slow digital push. It is often more stable.

10 · Quality gates

You're done when...

Tick every box before striking the set.

Image quality gates
  • All 3 stills are in ProRAW MAX (48MP, .dng file extension)
  • The X-Rite reference frame was shot first and is labelled ref-01.dng
  • White balance is consistent across all frames (colour picker on the sleeve should return the same RGB values)

  • Headline space above the container is at least 25% of the frame
  • Offer space below is at least 20% of the frame

  • Video is 3 to 4 seconds long (we'll trim the handles in post)

  • Exposure matches the straight-on still (same key and fill settings)

11 · Mistakes

Common mistakes and how to rescue them

The six traps that will kill a C4 shoot, and how to get out of them.

Mixed colour temperatures

Key is at 5600K but the fill drifted to 4800K — you didn't notice until reviewing on a laptop. The container sleeve now reads orange on one side and green on the other.

RescueRe-shoot the fill-side frames. You have time — the key is still locked. Do not try to "fix in post" across three different stills. It takes longer than a 10-minute re-shoot.
Tripod nudged between frames

Someone brushed the tripod between still 2 and still 3. The framing shifted by 2cm and the close-up crop no longer matches the hero.

RescueRe-shoot still 3 from scratch. The close-up is only one frame — you can afford the 3-minute reset.
Overexposed sleeve

You didn't pull the sun slider down one stop. The white areas of the sleeve are blown out and cannot be recovered.

RescueDrop exposure in ProCamera (drag the sun slider down to -1.3) and re-shoot. ProRAW can recover about a stop of highlight detail but cannot recover "pure white".
Garnish wilted

The parsley was fine when Kaja plated it 20 minutes ago. By the time you shot still 3, it had curled and browned under the key light.

RescueFresh sprig, 30 seconds. Always keep a backup garnish in the fridge during a C4 shoot. Never shoot still 3 with styled greens older than 15 minutes.
Reflection of the camera in the sleeve

The Chefly sleeve has a glossy laminated finish. The iPhone lens is now visible as a small dark rectangle on the foreground edge of the container.

RescueTilt the container 3 to 5 degrees toward camera-right so the glossy face angles the reflection away from the lens. If the reflection persists, add a piece of black card between the camera and the container below frame to kill the specular.
Backdrop crease

The ivory linen has a horizontal crease behind the container. At 48MP it will dominate the frame and distract from the product.

RescueIron the linen (steam iron, lowest setting). If no iron: re-pin tight and angle a light into the backdrop to wash out subtle shadows. Do not try to remove creases in Lightroom — the pixel clone will show in any social feed at 100% zoom.
12 · Post

Post-production handoff

What you hand over and what happens to it next.

C4 has the cleanest handoff of any format we shoot. Three stills, one video, one reference card, one folder. That's it.

Exports

  • Transfer all ProRAW .dng files from the phone to the iMac via AirDrop or Files. Do not convert to JPEG on the phone.
  • Drop the video into the same folder as the original 4K .mov file. Again, no pre-compression.
  • Include the reference frame and the colour checker frame. The editor snaps white balance from those.

Folder structure

  • Folder: /shoots/c4-offer-static/2026-04-19/
  • Files: ref-01.dng, still-01-hero.dng (x3), still-02-angle.dng (x3), still-03-closeup.dng (x3), video-01-pushin.mov
  • Naming: underscores no spaces, stamp the shoot date, keep the naming exactly this way

What happens next

  • Editor opens the reference frame in Lightroom, pulls the white balance eyedropper on the grey square of the X-Rite card
  • That WB is synced across all 9 stills. Colour-grade is minor — maybe +10 warmth and a touch of clarity
  • Stills go into the Meta Ads library as image variants for paid campaigns
  • Video gets a slow digital push or a gentle zoom-in effect in Final Cut, trimmed to 3s, and pushed as a video asset
13 · Self-check

Self-check quiz (5 questions)

Close the tab and answer from memory. Open the answer only after you've committed.

C4 self-check — you should score 5/5 before running this format solo
Why do we shoot in ProRAW and not standard JPEG/HEIC?
Reveal answer
ProRAW captures roughly 50x more colour data at 48MP. This matters because the editor is going to push shadow detail, warm the colour, and overlay bright offer text. A JPEG file would fall apart under those adjustments and show banding in the sleeve gradient. ProRAW carries the detail through grading.
Why does the reference colour checker frame go first?
Reveal answer
Because every later frame anchors its white balance to this one. In Lightroom the editor eyedroppers the grey card, then syncs that WB across all nine hero stills. If you forget to shoot it, each still has to be colour-matched by eye — about 30 minutes of unnecessary post work per still.
What is the flag doing in our light recipe?
Reveal answer
The black A4 card flag sits between the fill light and the camera. Its job is to stop the fill from hitting the front face of the container, so the front face is lit by the key only. This creates a clean directional shadow on the container that signals "product" rather than "lit object".
Why don't we move the tripod between still 1 and still 2?
Reveal answer
So the editor can A/B test them. When the camera is in exactly the same position, the two stills differ only by the angle of the container. The editor can overlay one on top of the other in the ad account, run them against each other, and attribute any performance difference purely to the composition change. If the camera has moved, other variables sneak in.
Why do we turn off iPhone stabilisation for the push-in video?
Reveal answer
Because the camera is already locked on a tripod, so there is no shake to correct. Stabilisation kicks in and introduces a subtle digital drift: it thinks it is correcting motion that isn't there, and warps the frame slightly. On a 3-second push-in that drift reads as a wobble in the edit, which is the one thing the format must not have.
