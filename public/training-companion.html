Part 0

Before shoot day

Do this the night before. Twenty minutes. Saves two hours of chaos in the morning.

Charge everything

  • iPhone 17 Pro Max (both units) to 100%. Check free storage, should be at least 40GB free each.
  • iPhone 15 Pro Max to 100%. Also 40GB free.
  • DJI Mic 2 TX (the clip-on), RX (the receiver), and the charging case. Case first, then the mics in the case.
  • DJI Osmo Mobile 6 gimbal (you'll barely use it, but charge it anyway).
  • Amaran 200x × 2. These usually run off mains, but confirm the power cables are packed.

Test audio 24 hours before

Plug the DJI Mic 2 RX into the iPhone you plan to use as A-cam. Record 30 seconds of yourself talking. Play it back on headphones. If you hear static, crackle, or drop-outs, it's a connection issue. Fix this the night before, not ten minutes before the first take.

Clean the kitchen counter

This is free production value. Clear everything off the surface, wipe it down, put back only what the sheet says should be in frame. An iPhone lens picks up every crumb.

Pre-cook one meal

For C3 and C5 you'll need a warm meal that steams when you peel the lid. Heat a container of food just before the first take so it actually produces visible steam. Cold food looks flat on camera.

Outfit check

Both of you: plain tops, no busy patterns, no logos, no stripes. Mid-tones (grey, dusty blue, olive, cream) photograph best. Avoid pure white (blows out) and pure black (swallows the face).

Part 1

Camera angles and framing

Every angle word that appears on a shoot sheet, explained in plain english with a diagram and an example.

The vocabulary you need to know

Shot sheets use twelve or so angle words on loop. Once you know these, the rest of production language stops being intimidating. Here they are, grouped by what they describe.

Where the camera is (position)

Straight-on / dead-on

Directly in front

Camera points straight at the subject's face. Lens, nose, and the back of the head would line up.

In C2 the A-cam tripod sits directly in front of James. He looks slightly to one side so he's not staring down the lens.
Three-quarter / 3/4 angle

Partway around to the side

Camera is roughly 45° off dead-on. You see most of the face but also part of the side (cheek, jaw, ear). It's halfway between straight-on and full profile.

"3/4 to Kaja's right shoulder" means stand to Kaja's right (her right, not yours) and shoot her face at an angle. You can see her full face and a bit of her right ear.
Side / profile

Full side view

Camera is 90° off dead-on. You see one full cheek, jaw, ear. Only one eye visible. Less common in our pack.

C1 shot 3 (cut-away): Kaja looking out of a window, camera captures her side profile. Gives you a silent cut-point.
Over-shoulder / over-the-shoulder

Behind, past the shoulder

Camera sits just behind one of the subject's shoulders, looking past it at what they're doing. Shows a bit of the back of the shoulder and what they're looking at.

C2 shot 3 B-roll: James opens his laptop. Camera is just behind James's right shoulder so we see the shoulder, the screen, and his hands. Feels like we're standing with him.
Overhead / top-down / bird's-eye

Straight down from above

Camera points directly down at the table or surface. You see the meal from the angle of someone standing over it. This is what the ARKON Pro Stand is for.

C5 is entirely overhead. The ARKON arm holds the iPhone above a chopping board, lens pointing at the floor. You reach in from the side to peel lids and pour sauce.
POV / point-of-view

First-person, through your eyes

Camera shows what you would be looking at if you were the person in the scene. On the neck mount, the lens sits on your chest and points where your eyes would look if they were tilted down at your hands.

C3 is POV throughout. Kaja wears the SmallRig neck mount. When she opens the fridge, we see her hands reaching into the fridge, exactly as she would see it. No face in frame.

How high the camera is (height)

Four common heights
Lens pointed straight out at the subject's level. Eye level = neutral. Chest height = slightly up the body. Low = dramatic, looks up at the person.
Top-down view of angles around you
If you drew a clock on the floor around the subject, 6 o'clock is straight-on, 3 and 9 are side, 4:30 and 7:30 are the 3/4 angles.

How close the camera is (shot size)

Wide / wide shot

Whole body plus room

You see the full person from head to feet and a lot of the room around them. Rarely used in our pack.

Medium / mid-shot

Roughly waist up

Head, shoulders, arms, down to the belt or hips. Good for someone talking while doing something with their hands.

C2 A-cam. James's head and shoulders and the top of the desk are all visible.
Chest-framed

Shoulders up to head

Tighter than medium. Viewer sees head, neck, tops of shoulders. Intimate, conversational.

C1 primary angle. Camera close enough that Kaja fills most of the 9:16 frame from the shoulder line up. Feels like she's talking directly to you.
Close-up

Just the face, or just a hand

The frame is filled with one small area: a face, a hand, a fork. Used for B-roll beats where the texture matters.

C2 shot 4: close-up on James's hand holding a container. Frame is just hand, container, bit of sleeve.
Macro / extreme close-up

One detail, very tight

Like close-up but even closer. Single ingredient, steam coming off a meal, one grain of rice. Not used in our pack, but you'll see it in studio work.

What the camera is doing (movement)

Handheld

You're holding the phone, by hand

You grip the phone with both hands (one on each side of it, tucked close to your chest). Small natural movement is fine and even useful for UGC. Don't shake or pan deliberately.

C1: James holds iPhone 17 Pro Max handheld, elbows tucked, shoulder-width stance. Small natural sway.
Tripod / static

Phone locked on a stand, not moving

Phone clamped to a tripod. Frame doesn't move at all. Used when you need clean, steady footage for dialogue or static shots.

C2 A-cam. The tripod holds the iPhone 15 Pro Max at eye level in front of James's desk. Camera never moves.
Push-in

Camera moves closer during the shot

Start wide, slowly walk or glide the camera forward, end tighter on the subject. In our pack: walk the phone forward 50cm over 3 seconds. No gimbal needed at that speed.

C4 shot 4: start with whole container in frame, slowly walk forward until the label fills the frame.
Pan / tilt

Camera rotates on a fixed spot

Pan = horizontal rotation (like turning your head left-right). Tilt = vertical rotation (like nodding your head up-down). Not used in our pack, but you'll hear the terms.

Pull focus

Focus shifts from one thing to another

The camera doesn't move but what's sharp changes. Used for dramatic reveals. iPhone does this automatically if you tap a new object. Don't worry about it in our pack.

Slow-mo / 120fps

Captured at high frame rate, plays back slow

Phone records more frames per second, so when played at normal speed it looks slow. In iPhone camera app: slide to "Slo-Mo" mode. We don't use slow-mo in this pack.

About focus / "shallow depth"

Plain englishWhen a shot sheet says "shallow depth of field" or "soft background", it means the thing in front is sharp and the background is blurry. On iPhone 17 Pro Max, switch to Portrait mode in the camera app to get this automatically. Tap the thing you want sharp. Works for people, food, objects.

About resolution / "ProRAW" / "4K60"

Plain englishProRAW = a still-image format that keeps more colour and detail data. Used for stills in C4 and C5. In iPhone camera app: settings → Formats → ProRAW switched on. Then in camera, tap "RAW" icon to enable per-shot. 4K60 = video resolution and frame rate. iPhone default is usually fine; if it's set lower, go to Settings → Camera → Record Video → 4K at 30 fps (our pack doesn't need 60).
Part 2

Lighting basics

What the lighting instructions mean, and how to tell if a setup is working.

The three words you need

  • Key light. The main light hitting the subject's face or the food. Does most of the work. We use the Amaran 200x as the key in every concept.
  • Fill light. A second, softer light that fills in the shadow side so faces don't look half-in-darkness. We use the Lantern 26" or the ARKON ring light as fill.
  • Modifier. A cloth or dome you put in front of a hard light to make it soft. The Dome III is a softbox you clip onto the Amaran. Light through it comes out wide and gentle instead of a laser beam.

What "camera-left 45°" means

Imagine a clock laid flat on the floor with the subject at the centre and the camera at 6 o'clock. "Camera-left 45°" means the light is at roughly 4 or 5 o'clock from the subject's point of view. It's hitting the subject's face diagonally from the front-left.

Classic setup: key camera-left 45°, fill camera-right
Key on one side, fill on the other, both slightly forward of the subject. The subject's face gets one directional shadow plus gentle fill on the dark side. Used for C1, C2.
Overhead food setup (C5)
Key light 45° high from one side, small fill on the other to stop shadows being too dark. Camera points straight down via ARKON arm.

Colour temperature (Kelvin / "4500K", "5600K")

Light has a colour. 3000K = warm/orange (candlelight). 5600K = cool/blue-white (daylight). The Amaran lets you set this with a dial. Match your light to the room. If you're shooting indoors with daylight through a window, set to around 5200K to 5600K. If the room has warm indoor lamps, set around 3800K to 4500K. Our pack uses 4500K to 5600K throughout.

How to tell if lighting is working

  • Face test: can you see both eyes clearly and catch a small reflection of the light in each eye? Good.
  • Shadow test: does the shadow side of the face still have some detail, or is it black? If it's black, the fill is too weak. Bring the Lantern in closer or turn it up.
  • Food test: is there one clear direction of shadow across the plate? Good. Shadow everywhere and food looks flat? Move the key further to the side.
Do
  • Place the key roughly 1.5 to 2m from the subject
  • Use the Dome III on the Amaran for soft, flattering light
  • Match your colour temperature to the room's existing light
  • Turn lights off and on once to check you can see the change on camera
Don't
  • Point the Amaran bare at a face without the Dome III. Too harsh.
  • Mix warm lamps with cool key. Turn background lamps off.
  • Place a light directly behind the camera. Flat and lifeless.
  • Change light position mid-shoot if the angle is working.
Part 3

Audio basics

The DJI Mic 2 setup in order. If audio works, everything else is fixable. If audio fails, the take is dead.

The parts in your DJI case

  • TX (transmitter). The small square with a clip. This goes on the person talking.
  • RX (receiver). The larger square. This connects to the iPhone.
  • Case. Charges all three.

Setup, step by step

  1. Open the case. TX and RX should both show 3 or 4 green dots = charged.
  2. Take the RX out. Plug it into the iPhone's USB-C port directly. It should power on and show a signal bar.
  3. Take the TX out. It pairs automatically with the RX (you'll see both show the same channel). If not, hold the pair button on both for 2 seconds.
  4. Clip the TX onto the talent's collar or shirt, under the collar if possible to hide it from the camera. The tiny hole on the top of the TX is the mic — that should face upward toward the talent's mouth.
  5. Open the iPhone camera app, switch to video mode. You should see a small audio bar or icon indicating external mic is active. If the iPhone still prompts to use the internal mic, reconnect the RX.
  6. Ask talent to talk at normal volume for 15 seconds. Record it. Play it back on headphones. Clear and no static = you're good. Crackle = unplug RX, wipe both connectors with a dry cloth, plug back in.

Where to clip the TX

Plain englishClip the TX about 15 to 20cm below the mouth (roughly where a tie pin would sit). Hidden under the collar is best. Don't clip onto a flappy bit of fabric (crunching sounds). Don't clip on anything that will rub against the TX (jewellery, another fabric layer).

What to listen for

  • Wind. If filming near a window or outside, put the fluffy foam cover (dead cat) on the TX. Removes wind noise.
  • Clothing rustle. Ask talent to stop moving their hands across their chest. Fabric against TX = ruined audio.
  • Room echo. Bare kitchen with hard surfaces can echo. If it sounds hollow, add soft things to the room (blanket over a chair off-camera, closed doors, fabric bag on the counter out-of-frame).

Why we only have 1 RX for 2 potential mics

Our current kit is 2× TX + 1× RX. That means one person at a time can be mic'd up to the iPhone. For C1 and C2 (one person talks at a time), this is fine. For any concept with two people talking at once (not in this pack), you'd need a second RX. We're not filming any two-person dialogue in the internal pack, so the single RX is enough.

If the DJI fails

Fallback order: (1) use the iPhone's onboard mic, but keep the iPhone within 50cm of the talker's face. (2) record audio on a second iPhone right next to talent, sync in post by clapping hands once at the start of each take.

Part 4

How to read a shoot sheet

The 9-column grid in the shoot pack, translated.

Column by column

  1. ✓ Tick box. Tick it when the shot is captured and reviewed.
  2. # Shot number. Order within this concept.
  3. Action / description. What actually happens on camera. The bold bit is the headline. The rest is detail.
  4. Frame / angle. Where the camera is and how close. E.g. "Handheld, chest height, 3/4 to right shoulder."
  5. Dur. How long this clip needs to run (not how long to film it — film longer to have safety).
  6. Audio / sound. What's recorded or added. "DJI Mic on" = someone is mic'd. "Foley" = natural real-world sound (peeling lid, fork clink).
  7. Talent. Who is in the frame. "Hands only" = no face visible.
  8. Props / set. What needs to be in the frame before you roll.
  9. Notes. The most important column. Read every word of this on the day.

Do you film shots 1 and 2 at the same time, or one after the other?

This is the single biggest source of confusion. The answer depends on whether both cameras can see what they need to see without getting in each other's frame.

Simultaneous (both cameras rolling at once)

If the camera for shot 1 is in one position and the camera for shot 2 is in a different position, and both can roll without appearing in each other's frame, then both run at the same time. Talent only performs once. We cut between angles in post.

In our pack: C2 runs this way. A-cam on tripod straight-on, B-cam on the ARKON clamp at a 3/4 side angle. Both record at once. James delivers his monologue once. Editor has two angles.

Sequential (one after the other, talent repeats)

If there's only one camera, or the two angles would put a camera in-frame of the other, you film one shot first, reset, then film the next. Talent repeats the take. Takes twice as long.

In our pack: C1 runs this way. Only one camera (handheld by James). Kaja delivers the monologue once handheld chest-framed, then again handheld at a 3/4 angle. Same script, different angle.

Section rows

When you see a highlighted row that says something like Take one · full monologue straight through or Cover · cut-aways and reaction beats, it's a section break, not a shot you film. It tells you the shots below it belong to one chunk of the day.

"Film separately, combine in post"

When you see this note, it means: the final video looks like things are happening at the same time (e.g. a split-screen), but you film each side one at a time. An editor puts them together on a timeline later.

"B-roll" vs "A-roll"

A-roll = the main footage, usually the person talking. B-roll = everything else the editor cuts to: hands, close-ups, action, reactions. Every dialogue concept needs B-roll so the editor can hide cuts in the A-roll.

"Foley"

Real-world sound captured at the source: peel of a lid, fork on plate, microwave beep. These are almost always captured by the DJI TX or the iPhone onboard mic. If the take is for a silent scene, the editor will add foley later from a library. In our pack, try to capture real foley on set.

Part 6

Common mistakes and rescues

Things that go wrong on a first shoot, and how to unblock without panicking.

Symptom
  • Audio sounds crackly or cuts out
  • Faces look half-dark, one side in shadow
  • Food looks flat, grey, no colour
  • Shaky handheld footage
  • Kaja's / James's delivery feels wooden
  • Container label is readable but out of focus
Rescue
  • Unplug DJI RX, wipe both ports dry, plug back in. If still bad, fall back to iPhone onboard mic and stay within 50cm of talent.
  • Bring the fill light closer, turn it up, or reposition key slightly forward of 45°.
  • Raise colour temperature slightly (cooler makes food pop more), move key light 15cm closer.
  • Tuck both elbows into your chest. Exhale before hitting record. Move your feet not your arms.
  • Scrap the script, have them explain the idea in their own words for 60 seconds. Use that take, even if messy.
  • Tap the label on the iPhone screen to focus on it. Repeat the shot.

If you run out of time

Priority order for the pack: C1 first (the emotional testimonial), C2 second (the founder angle). If you only capture C1 and C2 cleanly with good audio, you have enough for a week of paid social tests. C3, C4, C5 can all be shot on a second day.

If you're not sure a take is good enough

Film one more. Disk space is free. Time on set is not (but you're home, so it's fine). The only real cost is Kaja's or James's energy, so give each other a break between takes.

After the shoot

AirDrop or upload everything into one folder on the day. Name it 2026-04-19 internal pack raw. Don't edit on shoot day. Sleep, then watch it back the next morning with fresh eyes. Edits feel very different 12 hours later.
