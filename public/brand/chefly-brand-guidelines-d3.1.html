01

brand overview.

Chefly is a UK DTC meal delivery brand built for people who care about eating well but don't have the time or energy to cook from scratch every night. The D2 system — Light & Bold — is an evolution of the original Chefly identity, not a replacement. It keeps everything that made Chefly distinctive (the handwritten "ly", food-colour coding, lowercase confidence) and pushes the energy, typography, and sophistication forward.

The D2 philosophy is captured in one rule: 95% cream and black, 5% bright colour. Cream dominates every surface. Black type does the heavy lifting. Colour punctuates — it never floods. This creates a calm, premium canvas where food photography and bold typography can breathe, while the colour system retains its practical function of coding meals by protein type.

brand personality

Confident but never arrogant. Warm but not childish. Bold but not loud. Chefly speaks like a friend who happens to be a great cook — direct, relaxed, occasionally funny, always honest.

02

sacred layer.

Some elements define Chefly at a molecular level. They never change, regardless of where or how the brand appears. Everything else can evolve.

Sacred — never change

  • Always lowercase
  • Full stop at end of headlines
  • Left-aligned (never centred body text)
  • Food-colour associations (orange=beef, yellow=poultry, green=veg, blush=pork, blue=fish)
  • The handwritten "ly" element in the logo
  • The logo itself — unchanged
  • Cream/warm-white as the primary background

Evolve — can change

  • Colour saturation and brightness
  • Number of colours per layout
  • Typography selections
  • Emphasis devices (underlines → serif italic)
  • Photography style and direction
  • Texture treatments (grain, etc.)
  • Badge/label styling
  • Motion and animation approach
03

the logo.

The Chefly logo stays exactly as it is. The "chef" in bold sans and the handwritten "ly" script is the single most recognisable element of the brand. The D2 system is designed to work around and elevate the logo, not compete with it.

Primary usage

Black logo on cream or white backgrounds. This is the default for 95% of applications.

Reversed

Cream/white logo on near-black or food-colour backgrounds. Use sparingly — only when the 5% colour moment demands it.

Clear space

Minimum clear space around the logo equals the height of the "c" in "chef". No elements, badges, or text should intrude on this space.

Minimum size

The logo should never be reproduced smaller than 24mm wide in print or 80px wide on screen. The "ly" script must remain legible.

The ® mark. Always include the registered trademark symbol at first use on any page, document, or packaging surface. Position it as a superscript to the right of the "ly" script.

the 95/5 rule

Every layout, every surface, every touchpoint: 95% of the visible area is cream/warm-white background with near-black type. The remaining 5% is a single food colour used as punctuation — a bar, a badge, a highlight, an accent line. Never more than one food colour per layout unless you are showing the full protein range (e.g. a menu grid).

07

emphasis system.

The old Chefly brand used hand-drawn circles and underlines to draw attention to key words. D2 replaces these with the serif/sans swap: one word in a Syne bold headline is set in Instrument Serif italic. This does the same job — pulls the eye to the important word — but with more sophistication and without the production cost of hand-drawn elements.

real food, zero effort.
chef-prepared meals delivered.
eat well without the work.

rules

One emphasis word per headline — never two or more. The emphasis word should be the emotional or action word in the phrase, not the subject. Place it toward the end when possible. Always include the full stop after the emphasis word if it ends the headline.

colour treatment

On digital surfaces (website, app, marketing emails, social graphics, documents), the emphasis word takes Chefly Orange (#FF6B2C). This creates a double signal — the font swap catches your eye, the colour confirms it's intentional. On dark backgrounds, the emphasis word shifts to cream (#FFF6EE).

On packaging labels, the emphasis word stays near-black. Sleeve stickers are printed per-batch as meals change, and adding a second ink colour for one word isn't practical. The Instrument Serif swap alone carries the signature at packaging scale — no colour needed.

digital — orange emphasis
eat well without the work.
packaging — black emphasis
with roasted carrots and sweet potato.

one word. every time.

The single italic word is chefly's typographic signature. It's the visual equivalent of a chef placing the final garnish — one deliberate gesture that elevates everything around it. The moment you use two, the effect disappears.

usage rules

Badges sit in horizontal rows with 8px gaps between them. On packaging sleeves, a maximum of three badges per row to avoid clutter. On digital surfaces, badge rows can wrap but should not exceed two rows. Never stack badges vertically. Badge text is always a short phrase — two to three words maximum.

On packaging. Badge backgrounds should be printed as a spot colour or at 100% opacity — never screened back. On kraft or textured stock, use the outline variant to avoid colour matching issues.
09

paper grain texture.

The paper grain is a subtle noise layer applied over cream backgrounds on digital surfaces. It mimics the texture of uncoated paper stock, giving screens a tactile, physical warmth that pure flat colour can't achieve. Think of it as the difference between a smooth phone screen and holding a piece of quality stationery.

It's deliberately subtle. The point isn't to look textured — it's to feel warmer without the viewer consciously noticing. But it needs to be visible enough that the choice reads as intentional, not accidental.

Print / packagingn/aThe physical paper stock provides the texture. No digital grain needed — in fact, printing a noise pattern on already-textured stock looks muddy.

implementation

The grain is applied as a CSS pseudo-element (::before or ::after) using an inline SVG feTurbulence filter. It's positioned fixed to cover the full viewport, set to pointer-events: none so it doesn't interfere with interaction, and layered at a high z-index. This means the grain sits over everything, giving a consistent paper feel across the entire page.

Performance note. SVG noise filters are GPU-accelerated in modern browsers and add negligible performance cost. On older devices, the grain can be disabled entirely with a prefers-reduced-motion media query — the brand works perfectly without it.

10

colour usage modes.

There are four ways to use colour in D2 layouts. Most pieces should use Mode 1 (Cream Hero). The others exist for specific moments where more energy is needed.

mode 1: cream hero
95% cream bg + black type. Colour appears only in a badge, a thin bar, or a small accent element. This is the default for everything.
real food, zero effort.
mode 2: colour band
Cream background with a bold left-edge or top-edge colour bar (6px). The bar signals the food colour / protein type without flooding the layout.
thai green curry.
mode 3: clean white
Pure white background for UI-heavy contexts — menus, order flows, account pages. Cream accents are used for card backgrounds and hover states.
your next box.
mode 4: food colour
Full food-colour background with white or near-black type. Reserved for single-meal hero moments, packaging accents, and the 5% colour pops. Never for full layouts.
this week's special.
Mode 4 is the 5%. If you find yourself using a full food-colour background for more than one element in a layout, you've broken the 95/5 rule. Pull back to Mode 1 or 2.
11

photography direction.

Food photography is the hero of every Chefly touchpoint. The D2 system demands that photography is natural, warm, and appetising — never over-styled or artificially lit. The food should look like it was just plated in a real kitchen, not a studio.

principles

Natural light. Soft, directional daylight. No harsh studio lighting, no dramatic shadows. The light source should feel like a kitchen window.

Overhead and three-quarter angles. Overhead (flat lay) for grid/menu layouts. Three-quarter angle for hero shots where you want depth and appetite appeal.

Real surfaces. Wooden boards, linen, concrete counters, matte ceramics. No glossy surfaces, no pure white styling plates, no artificial props.

Steam and freshness. If the food is hot, show steam. If there are herbs, they should look freshly torn. The food should look alive, not arranged.

Minimal styling. One or two supporting elements maximum — a fork, a napkin, a lemon half. The food is the subject. Everything else is background.

motion principles.

Movement in D2 is quick, confident, and purposeful. Nothing floats or drifts. Elements snap in with gentle easing, like placing a plate on a table.

css implementation

Here are production-ready CSS snippets for the core motion patterns. Copy these directly into your stylesheet.

/* ── chefly fade-in on scroll ── */ .chefly-reveal { opacity: 0; transform: translateY(16px); transition: opacity 300ms ease-out, transform 300ms cubic-bezier(0.16, 1, 0.3, 1); } .chefly-reveal.visible { opacity: 1; transform: translateY(0); }

stagger timing formula

When multiple elements animate in sequence (e.g. a grid of meal cards), each child gets an incremental delay. The formula: base delay + (index × 60ms). Cap the maximum stagger at 6 items (360ms total spread) — after that, additional items animate simultaneously with the 6th.

/* ── stagger children ── */ .chefly-stagger > *:nth-child(1) { transition-delay: 0ms; } .chefly-stagger > *:nth-child(2) { transition-delay: 60ms; } .chefly-stagger > *:nth-child(3) { transition-delay: 120ms; } .chefly-stagger > *:nth-child(4) { transition-delay: 180ms; } .chefly-stagger > *:nth-child(5) { transition-delay: 240ms; } .chefly-stagger > *:nth-child(n+6) { transition-delay: 300ms; }

page load orchestration

On initial page load, elements animate in this order: (1) navigation fades in immediately, (2) hero headline reveals at 100ms, (3) hero supporting text at 200ms, (4) primary CTA at 350ms, (5) below-fold content animates on scroll via IntersectionObserver. Total load sequence completes within 500ms — fast enough to feel instant, slow enough to feel intentional.

reduced motion strategy

Users who set prefers-reduced-motion: reduce in their OS should get a fully functional experience with no animation. This is a legal and ethical requirement, not a nice-to-have.

/* ── respect user motion preferences ── */ @media (prefers-reduced-motion: reduce) { *, *::before, *::after { animation-duration: 0.01ms !important; animation-iteration-count: 1 !important; transition-duration: 0.01ms !important; scroll-behavior: auto !important; } .chefly-reveal { opacity: 1; transform: none; } }
Scroll-triggered animations. Use IntersectionObserver (threshold: 0.15) to add the .visible class when elements enter the viewport. Never use scroll-hijacking, parallax, or scroll-linked animations that affect page scrolling behaviour. The user controls the scroll — chefly controls the reveal.

video motion

For video content (ads, social, Ken Burns clips): cuts are fast (0.8–1.2s per clip in hooks, 2–3s in body). Text overlays use Syne Extra Bold, always lowercase, and animate in from the left or bottom. No spinning, no bouncing, no 3D rotations. The tone is editorial, not energetic.

13

tone of voice.

Always lowercase. Headlines, badges, CTAs — everything is lowercase. This is Chefly's most recognisable verbal tic. It signals relaxed confidence. The only exception is the ® mark after Chefly.

Full stop to close. Every headline ends with a full stop. It creates a sense of finality and quiet authority. "real food, zero effort." feels more assured than "real food, zero effort".

Short, direct sentences. Chefly talks like a good friend, not a copywriter. "ready in 5 minutes." beats "Our meals are designed for convenience and can be prepared in as little as five minutes." Say it once, say it clearly, move on.

Warm, not cute. Chefly has personality but doesn't try too hard. No puns. No food wordplay ("we're on a roll!"). No exclamation marks unless genuinely excited. Humour is dry, understated, and rare.

example phrases

dinner sorted. every night.
chef-prepared. your kitchen.
skip the meal prep. keep the taste.
14

do's and don'ts.

Do

Use cream as the dominant background on every surface. Let the food and type do the work.

Don't

Flood a layout with food colour. If more than 5% of the visible area is a bright colour, pull back.

Do

Use one Instrument Serif italic word per headline to draw the eye.

Don't

Italicise multiple words, or use the serif font for body text. It's an accent, not a workhorse.

Do

Use badges to communicate functional info quickly — protein, dietary, calories, status.

Don't

Stack more than three badges in a row on packaging, or use badges for marketing slogans.

Do

Apply paper grain texture to digital backgrounds at 4–6% opacity for warmth.

Don't

Apply grain to video overlays or print. Video doesn't need it; print paper provides its own texture.

Do

Keep everything lowercase, including CTAs and navigation labels.

Don't

Use title case, all caps, or sentence case anywhere in the brand. Lowercase is sacred.

Do

End every headline with a full stop. It's a brand signature.

Don't

Use exclamation marks, ellipses, or question marks in headlines. The full stop is the only acceptable terminator.

16

sleeve design.

The sleeve surface is the centrepiece of Chefly's packaging identity. Rather than commissioning bespoke illustrations per recipe, Chefly uses a tonal ingredient motif system: a reusable library of ~20 simple line-drawn ingredient SVGs (herbs, vegetables, grains, proteins) rendered in a lighter or darker tone of the sleeve's food-colour. This gives each sleeve visual depth and recipe-specific character without an ongoing illustrator dependency.

the motif library

Design asset, not illustration. The motif library is a set of ~20 ingredient SVGs: broccoli, chilli, garlic, lemon, carrot, sweet potato, rice, noodles, rosemary, thyme, chickpeas, aubergine, kale, apple, sage, peppercorns, couscous grains, lentils, onion, and ginger. Each is a simple single-stroke line drawing, designed to work at 10–20% opacity on any food-colour background. A designer builds these once; every new recipe picks 4–6 motifs and arranges them across the sleeve's colour zone. No illustrator per SKU, no commissioning cycle, no bottleneck.

how it works in practice

On darker backgrounds (orange, sky blue), motifs are white at low opacity. On lighter backgrounds (yellow, green, blush), motifs are near-black at low opacity. The effect is subtle — you notice it when you look closely, but from a distance the solid food-colour dominates. This is the Aesop principle: the system does the work, not individual artwork. The motif approach. The SVG mockups above use simplified ingredient motifs from the reusable library. In production, a designer selects 4–6 motifs relevant to each recipe's ingredients and arranges them across the sleeve surface. motif rules

Do

Use simple, single-stroke line art. Each motif should feel organic and hand-drawn — slight imperfections are welcome. Keep motifs recognisable at small scale.

Don't

Use filled shapes, gradients, multiple stroke weights, photography, clip-art, or overly detailed illustrations. Motifs should be line-only, single stroke weight, and work at 10–20% opacity without becoming muddy.

evaluating motif quality

When reviewing new motif additions to the library, use these adjective pairs to assess whether the work "feels chefly." If a motif falls on the right side of any pair, redraw it toward the left.

brand checklist.

Run through this before shipping anything — ad, social post, packaging, email, webpage.

general

  • Is everything lowercase?
  • Does every headline end with a full stop?
  • Is the layout at least 95% cream/white with near-black type?
  • Is colour used as a 5% accent, not a flood?
  • Does the headline have exactly one Instrument Serif italic emphasis word?
  • Are badges using the correct type (food colour / status / functional / nutritional)?
  • Is the badge text lowercase, max 2–3 words?
  • Is the paper grain applied at the right opacity for the medium?
  • Is the grain disabled for video and print?
  • Does the food photography use natural light on real surfaces?
  • Is the logo at minimum size (24mm print / 80px screen) with correct clear space?
  • Does the copy sound like a friend, not a copywriter?
  • Is the type hierarchy clear: Syne display → Instrument accent → Space Grotesk body?
  • Are food-colour associations correct (orange=beef, yellow=poultry, green=veg, blush=pork, blue=fish)?

digital components & UX states

  • Are buttons using the correct hierarchy (primary/secondary/ghost/danger)?
  • Do all form fields show focus, error, and disabled states?
  • Is every empty state designed with an icon, headline, and helpful action?
  • Do error messages follow the formula: what happened + why + what to do?
  • Are loading states showing progress feedback with a helpful message?
  • Do success confirmations tell the user what happens next?
  • Are toast notifications using the correct colour (green for success, red for error)?

accessibility & user preferences

  • Does all text-on-colour meet WCAG AA contrast ratios (4.5:1 for body, 3:1 for large text)?
  • Do food-colour badges include a protein icon alongside the text label?
  • Is the paper grain overlay set to aria-hidden="true"?
  • Can every interactive element be reached and activated via keyboard?
  • Do focus indicators use the 2px near-black outline with 2px offset?
  • Are all touch targets at least 44×44px on mobile?
  • Does the site work with prefers-reduced-motion: reduce?
  • Do error messages use role="alert" for screen readers?
  • Does prefers-color-scheme: dark apply the warm-down palette (not a dark theme)?
  • Does forced-colors: active (Windows High Contrast) add visible borders?

responsive & power-user

Accessibility isn't a feature — it's a requirement. Roughly 1 in 12 male customers has some form of colour vision deficiency, and every customer benefits from clear contrast, readable text, and keyboard-navigable interfaces. chefly targets WCAG 2.1 AA compliance across all digital surfaces.

The rule. If text needs to be read, use near-black (#0D0D0D) on any food-colour background. The only exception is white text on orange for large headlines (≥24px bold). When in doubt, go darker.

non-colour badge signals

The food-colour badge system works brilliantly for most users, but ~8% of men have red-green colour vision deficiency and may struggle to distinguish the orange (beef) badge from the green (veg) badge. To ensure every customer can identify their meals, every food-colour badge must include a small protein icon alongside the text label.

accessible badge format — colour + icon + text
🥩 beef 🍗 poultry 🌱 veg 🐖 pork 🐟 fish
Each badge uses three redundant signals: colour + icon + text label. If any one signal is missed, the other two carry the meaning.
icon: 14px circle · emoji or simple SVG glyph · positioned left of text · gap: 6px
On packaging. Printed badges should include the protein name as text on every badge (which they already do). On the website, add the icon as an additional signal. The text label is the primary identifier; colour and icon are reinforcement.

keyboard navigation

Every interactive element must be reachable via keyboard Tab and activatable via Enter/Space. Focus indicators use a 2px near-black outline with a 2px offset (so it doesn't touch the element). Never remove the outline — style it to match chefly instead.

/* ── chefly focus indicator ── */ :focus-visible { outline: 2px solid #0D0D0D; outline-offset: 2px; border-radius: 4px; } /* remove default outline only when :focus-visible is supported */ :focus:not(:focus-visible) { outline: none; }

minimum touch targets

All interactive elements on mobile must meet a 44×44px minimum touch target (WCAG 2.5.8). Badge buttons, card tap areas, and close buttons are the most common offenders. If the visual element is smaller than 44px, pad the tap area with invisible hit zones using CSS padding or pseudo-elements.

dark mode & high contrast

chefly stays light. The cream-dominant palette is a sacred brand decision — it's what makes chefly feel like a kitchen, not an app. When a user's operating system is set to dark mode, the chefly website does not switch to a dark theme.

the official position.

Respect the user's preference by acknowledging it, but maintain brand identity. The prefers-color-scheme: dark media query should slightly warm the palette — shifting cream from #FFF6EE to #F5EDE4 and white cards from #FFFFFF to #FAF5EF — to reduce glare on screens calibrated for dark environments. This is a subtle comfort adjustment, not a theme switch.

/* ── gentle warm-down for dark-mode users ── */ @media (prefers-color-scheme: dark) { :root { --cream: #F5EDE4; --warm-white: #FAF5EF; } } /* ── Windows High Contrast Mode ── */ @media (forced-colors: active) { .badge, .btn-chefly { border: 1px solid ButtonText; } .paper-grain, body::before { display: none; } .state-loading-bar .bar-fill { background: Highlight; } }
Never build a dark chefly. If a developer or agency asks for a dark mode, the answer is no. The cream palette is as sacred as the lowercase rule. The gentle warm-down above is the only permitted adaptation — it keeps the brand intact while reducing screen brightness for dark-mode users.
