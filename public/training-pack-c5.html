C5 · Top-down food hero

A 10-second overhead film plus three still frames, all of the same scene. Camera looks straight down. The container is centre-frame. Kaja's hands enter and exit to do 5 choreographed beats. Everything about this format is about appetite: colour, steam, texture, motion.

Priority 5 Video + 3 stills Overhead rig 4K 60fps 10s film · 5 beats ~45 min on set Difficulty: medium

Learning goals

  • Mount the ARKON Pro Stand overhead at 90 degrees perpendicular to the table
  • Light a top-down shot with an overhead key and a camera-side fill that doesn't cast the camera's shadow into the frame
  • Choreograph 5 hand-only beats (place, cutlery, garnish, slice, lift) in one continuous 10s take
  • Get three colour-matched stills from the same overhead position without rebuilding the set
  • Deliver a 10s film with satisfying motion at every beat and three hero stills ready for post

Pre-requisites

  • C1 to C4 complete. ARKON Pro Stand and overhead arm physically on set.
  • Read Training Companion Parts 2 (light), 3 (camera), and Part 5 §C5
  • iPhone 17 Pro Max charged past 40%. 4K 60fps tested on the phone beforehand.
  • Kaja has practiced the 5-beat sequence once off-camera — her hands know the rhythm

What you'll deliver

  • 1 x 10-second 4K 60fps overhead film showing 5 choreographed hand beats on the container
  • 3 ProRAW stills from the same overhead angle: full table, mid-plating, final served frame
  • 1 reference frame with the X-Rite card in-shot for white balance
  • All files transferred to the Drive folder, named and labelled, within 24 hours

01 · Concept

What we're making and why it works

Straight down. Hands do the work. Every beat sells appetite.

C5 is the top-down food hero: a cinematic overhead film that shows a Chefly meal becoming a proper meal. The camera never moves. The scene is built inside the frame by Kaja's hands entering and exiting. Each of the 5 beats is a micro-reveal: container lands, lid comes off, cutlery enters, garnish sprinkles, fork lifts. Total 10 seconds.

This is the format that Instagram and TikTok reward hardest. It combines three things the algorithms love: high-contrast colour, consistent framing, and human presence without face. The viewer fills in the blank with themselves. It is also the format that Meta ad creatives pull still-frame carousels from, so one shoot gives us five assets.

Plain-english summaryCamera above the table looking down. Kaja's hands come into frame, place the container, pop the lid, add a fork, sprinkle garnish, lift a forkful to camera. Camera rolls through all five beats as one 10-second shot. We also grab three stills at key beats.

Reference winners this pattern came from

  • Mindful Chef overhead reel — 12 seconds, 4 beats, earned 3.8M organic views. 85% completion rate.
  • Gousto hero film — 9 seconds, 5 beats, ran 6 months in paid. Top-performing creative of Q3.
  • Both ads succeeded because the overhead angle is inherently watchable. You can't scroll past a top-down shot of food appearing. The pattern interrupt is too strong.

03 · Framing

Framing, camera settings, and crop

Camera pointed straight down. Container centred. 4K 60fps so the editor can slow any beat.

The iPhone 17 Pro Max mounts on the ARKON Pro Stand, pointed straight down at the table. The arm extends about 45cm out from the base so the camera sits directly over the centre of the chopping board. Height is adjusted so the board fills roughly 80% of the 4:3 frame.

We shoot 4K 60fps. This is deliberate. Even though the final film plays at normal speed, shooting at 60fps gives the editor the option to slow any beat to 50% (becomes 30fps) or 40% (becomes 24fps cinematic slow-mo) without any visible judder. The steam rise and the fork lift are the two beats that usually get slowed.

Video settings:

  • Mode: Video → 4K at 60fps
  • Lens: 1x (main sensor)
  • HDR: OFF
  • Stabilisation: OFF
  • Aspect: 4:3 native (crop to 4:5 or 1:1 in post)
  • Exposure: tap container lid, lock with long-press, pull slider down 0.5 stops

Stills settings (after video wraps):

  • Mode: Photo → ProRAW MAX (48MP)
  • Aspect: 4:3
  • AE/AF lock on container lid
  • Pull exposure down one full stop — recover in post

04 · Lighting

Lighting setup, step by step

Key overhead and behind the camera. Fill at table height, camera-left. The camera must not cast its own shadow into the frame.

The hardest problem on a top-down shoot is the camera's own shadow. If the key light is directly over the table, the iPhone blocks it and casts a dark rectangle into the middle of the frame. The fix: offset the key behind the camera at ~60 degrees, so the light comes over the camera from a point slightly to the rear rather than from directly above.

  1. Mount the Amaran 200x + Dome III on a C-stand positioned behind and above the ARKON Pro Stand. The Dome III face should be angled forward-down toward the table, about 150cm above the board, hitting it at roughly 60 degrees off-vertical. Dial to 75%.
  2. Set the Lantern 26" on a mini stand at camera-left, at table height. The Lantern should be just slightly above the board surface, lighting across the scene rather than down into it. 5600K, 25%.
  3. Check for the camera shadow. Look at the iPhone's live preview. If you see a dark rectangle falling across the container, rotate the key light 10 to 15 degrees further behind the camera. The shadow should fall off the edge of the board, not onto it.
  4. Flag the ceiling. If the room's overhead lights are on, they will bleed warm tungsten into the top of your frame. Either turn them off completely, or clip a black flag between them and the table.
  5. White balance both lights to 5600K. Drift kills the hero still. Double-check with the X-Rite card.
  6. Pull exposure down 0.5 stops for video and 1 stop for stills. The oak board and sleeve both sit in mid-tones — under-exposure protects the highlights and gives the editor room to warm the image in grade.
Lighting plan (side view)

Lighting plan (top-down)

Do
  • Offset the key behind the camera by 60° so the camera shadow drops off the frame
  • Run both lights at 5600K
  • Shoot the X-Rite frame first and pull it out for takes
  • Shoot 4K 60fps for the film — gives slow-mo headroom
Don't
  • Put the key light directly above the camera — iPhone casts a rectangular shadow
  • Leave room overheads on — they bleed warm tungsten into the top of frame
  • Shoot 30fps — the fork-lift beat will not slow cleanly
  • Let the ARKON Pro Stand arm sag — counterweight it properly
05 · Audio

Audio: silent plus foley

No dialogue. Let the editor source foley in post — or quiet-record it on set.

There is no spoken word in a C5 film. The soundscape is entirely ambient and foley: the click of a fork on a board, the thunk of a container lid, the crinkle of a garnish being pinched. We have two options for audio, and they each have trade-offs.

Option A (default) · Record room silent, add foley in post

Roll with iPhone onboard mic only. The editor sources royalty-free foley from Epidemic Sound or records new foley on a quiet Sunday. This is the fast path. 80% of our C5 films use this workflow.

Option B · Live foley on set

After the hero takes, set up the DJI Mic 2 on a boom over the table (not chest-mounted). Do 3 clean foley passes per beat: container thunk, lid peel, fork click, cutlery scrape, and a loose "eating" sound of a forkful hitting the board. Takes 15 extra minutes. Worth it if the editor has flagged a specific sound they want hero.

On-set audio ruleIf the kitchen fan is on, the dishwasher is running, or there's traffic outside, do not do live foley. The ambient floor will be unusable. Close all the windows, kill the extractor fan, pause the playlist. Only then roll live foley.
06 · Wardrobe

Wardrobe, hands, and set dressing

Kaja's hands are the actors. They need to read warm, confident, and clean.

Kaja's hands

  • Nails short and natural. No polish, no gel, no chips. Buff the cuticle line.
  • Wash and moisturise 10 minutes before. Hands that look cracked or too pink read poorly on camera.
  • Rings off. Leave watches, bracelets, and the engagement ring off set.
  • Cuffs rolled. If Kaja is wearing sleeves, roll or push them above the wrist so only skin enters the frame.
  • No visible plaster or cut. If there's a cut on the back of the hand, reschedule the shot or frame around it.

Set dressing inside the frame

  • Oak chopping board — warm, lightly oiled, no visible knife marks. Long edge parallel to the camera's short axis.
  • Ivory linen runner — folded into one top corner of the frame to add texture without crowding.
  • One sprig of herb in a ramekin — sits off-frame in the top-right corner, ready for Kaja to pinch and sprinkle on beat 4.
  • A polished fork — sits off-frame bottom-right, ready to enter on beat 3.
  • Nothing else in shot. No phone, no cup, no napkin ring, no kitchen clutter. The frame is already busy with the five beats.
07 · Beat sheet

The 5-beat action sequence

One 10-second continuous take. Five choreographed beats. No cuts, no talking.

The film is a single 10-second overhead take. Kaja does five choreographed actions with her hands. The camera never moves. The editor can trim and pace later, but the whole film must exist in one continuous recording so the light stays consistent.

Before rolling, Kaja walks through the sequence off-camera once or twice so her hands know the rhythm. Then we roll and capture three takes of the full 10 seconds.

Beat 1
Place — Kaja's hands enter from the bottom of the frame, carrying the unopened Chefly container. They place it on the oak board, centre-frame, then withdraw.(0.0s to 2.0s)Place the container deliberately. No slide, no hesitation. The thunk on the board is the first audio beat.
Beat 2
Lid — Hands re-enter, peel the lid off the container in a single smooth motion, hold for half a second so the steam rises into frame, then exit the lid stage-left.(2.0s to 4.5s)The steam is the hero moment. Hot food, fresh off the microwave. If there is no steam, we have to reheat and try again.
Beat 3
Cutlery — Hand enters from bottom-right carrying the fork. Places the fork gently on the oak board at the 5 o'clock position relative to the container. Withdraws.(4.5s to 6.0s)Fork should make a quiet but audible click on the board. Polish the fork beforehand so it catches the key light.
Beat 4
Garnish — Hand enters from top-right with a pinch of parsley (or other green herb). Sprinkles it across the food with a small, deliberate motion. Withdraws.(6.0s to 7.5s)Pinch from 20cm above the container. The falling garnish is a classic food-ad beat — the audience knows the gesture and reads it as "care".
Beat 5
Lift — Hand re-enters from bottom-right, picks up the fork, spears a forkful of food, lifts it up toward the camera until the forkful is about 10cm below the lens. Hold. Cut.(7.5s to 10.0s)This is the payoff. The camera has watched a meal assemble itself, and now the forkful is coming to the viewer. Hold it long enough for the editor to freeze-frame if needed.

09 · Timing plan

Minute-by-minute timing plan

45 minutes on set. Rig first, light second, food last.

Minute00 to 08
Set the ARKON Pro Stand overhead. Counterweight on. Bubble level flat. Arm extended 45cm. Camera mounted on phone clamp. Centre the arm over the table.
Minute08 to 16
Set lighting. Amaran 200x + Dome III on C-stand behind camera, 150cm up, angled down at 60°. Lantern 26" camera-left at table height. Both at 5600K. Test for camera shadow — adjust key position until shadow falls off-frame.
Minute16 to 20
Camera settings. iPhone 17 Pro Max in 4K 60fps. Stabilisation OFF. HDR OFF. Airplane mode ON. Tap container lid to lock AE/AF. Pull sun slider down 0.5 stops.
Minute20 to 22
White balance test. X-Rite card on board, shoot one frame. Check colours on laptop. Adjust key/fill temperature if drifted.
Minute22 to 26
Rehearse the 5 beats. Kaja walks through Place → Lid → Cutlery → Garnish → Lift, off-camera. Two rehearsals. Confirms hand paths and timing.
Minute26 to 28
Microwave the container to 70 seconds. Hot. Fresh. Steam-ready. The moment the microwave pings, we roll.
Minute28 to 31
Take 1. Roll. 10 seconds continuous. Review on phone immediately. If steam was weak, reheat and re-shoot.
Minute31 to 34
Take 2. Reheat container 30 seconds. Fresh fork polish. Fresh garnish sprig. Roll. Review.
Minute34 to 37
Take 3 (safety). Only if takes 1 or 2 had issues. Reheat, fresh props, roll.
Minute37 to 42
Three stills. Switch iPhone to ProRAW MAX. Shoot full-table hero (container closed), mid-plating (lid off, fork on board), final served (garnish on, fork in food). 3 frames of each. Kaja styles between stills.
Minute42 to 45
Wrap and review. Scrub through every file on the laptop. Confirm WB match, focus clean, no camera shadow, all 5 beats readable in at least one take. Tear down.
10 · Quality gates

You're done when...

Tick every box before striking the set.

Video gates
  • At least one clean 10-second take where all 5 beats read clearly
  • Steam visible on Beat 2 (the lid comes off and steam rises into frame)
  • No camera shadow visible anywhere in the frame across the full take

  • Both lights are 5600K, key ~75%, fill ~25%
  • ARKON Pro Stand is rock-solid, no wobble on bumps

11 · Mistakes

Common mistakes and how to rescue them

The six traps that will kill a C5 shoot, and how to get out of them.

Camera shadow in frame

The key light is too close to directly above. The iPhone is blocking it and casting a rectangular shadow over the container.

RescueRotate the key light 10 to 15 degrees further behind the camera. The key should strike the board from the back, not the top. Watch the live preview as you adjust until the shadow falls off the edge.
No steam on Beat 2

The container cooled between microwave and roll. When Kaja pops the lid there is nothing rising.

RescueReheat the container for 30 seconds. Start rolling the moment the microwave pings. A container that sat for more than 60 seconds off the microwave will not steam visibly on camera.
Focus hunts during the take

You forgot to long-press the focus point and lock it. As Kaja's hands enter and exit the frame, the iPhone tries to refocus and pulls in and out.

RescueAlways long-press the AE/AF lock on the container lid before rolling. Confirm you see the yellow AE/AF lock banner at the top of the screen. If it is not locked, the camera will chase Kaja's hands.
Garnish falls on the sleeve edge, not the food

Kaja pinched from 10cm up. The sprinkle scattered wide and half landed on the container sleeve instead of the food.

RescueReshoot the take. Kaja pinches from 20cm up (not 10), and her fingers release directly above the centre of the food. The extra height gives the sprinkle room to fall concentrated rather than scatter.
ARKON Pro Stand wobbles

Someone knocked the base leg. The overhead arm is gently swaying. The film is unusable because the frame drifts.

RescueAdd a counterweight to the base leg (a bag of rice or a couple of books works). Re-check the arm is flat with a bubble level. Wait 30 seconds for any sway to dampen before rolling again.
Beats feel mechanical

Kaja's hands look rigid and pre-planned. The film reads staged rather than natural. The 60fps is exposing the stiffness.

RescueDo one more rehearsal off-camera at double-speed. Ask Kaja to pretend she's plating for a friend who's arriving in 10 minutes. The mild time-pressure warmth loosens the hand motion. Reshoot. Take 3 is almost always the keeper for this reason.
12 · Post

Post-production handoff

What you hand over and what happens to it next.

Exports

  • AirDrop all 3 video takes to the iMac. Keep them at original 4K 60fps resolution. Never pre-compress.
  • Transfer all ProRAW .dng stills.
  • Include the X-Rite reference frame.

Folder structure

  • Folder: /shoots/c5-topdown-hero/2026-04-19/
  • Files: ref-01.dng, video-01-take1.mov, video-02-take2.mov, video-03-take3.mov, still-01-full.dng (x3), still-02-midplating.dng (x3), still-03-served.dng (x3)
  • Naming: underscores no spaces, stamp the shoot date, keep the naming exactly this way

What happens next

  • Editor picks the hero take from the three video takes. Usually take 2 or 3.
  • Cut down to the final 10 seconds (trim any dead frames at start and end).
  • Add foley: container thunk, lid peel, fork click, herb sprinkle, eating beat.
  • Colour-grade warm (+12 warmth, +8 vibrance, -5 clarity for softness).
  • Export three crops: 9:16 for Reels and Shorts, 4:5 for feed, 1:1 for carousel.
  • Still frames become Meta ad image variants and Instagram single-image posts.

13 · Self-check

Self-check quiz (5 questions)

Close the tab and answer from memory. Open the answer only after you've committed.

C5 self-check — score 5/5 before running this format solo
Why do we position the key light behind the camera at 60 degrees, not directly above?
Reveal answer
So the iPhone does not cast its own shadow into the frame. If the key is directly overhead, the camera body blocks the light and a dark rectangle falls across the centre of the shot — right where the container is. Offsetting the key 60° behind the camera pushes that shadow off the edge of the board where it is invisible.
Why do we shoot the hero film at 4K 60fps and not 4K 30fps?
Reveal answer
So the editor can slow any beat to 50% or 40% without visible judder. Beat 2 (steam) and Beat 5 (fork lift) are the two beats most often slowed for dramatic effect. At 30fps source, slowing them halves the frame rate to 15fps, which stutters. At 60fps source, slowing to 50% still renders at 30fps, which plays smoothly.
Why does Kaja walk through the 5 beats off-camera before rolling?
Reveal answer
To get the rhythm into her hands without burning a take. C5 is a single continuous 10-second take with five distinct beats. Each beat needs clean timing so the whole take feels unforced. Rehearsing twice before rolling means her hands already know the path and timing, so Take 1 is much closer to usable than it would be cold.
Why do we microwave the container again between takes?
Reveal answer
Because the steam fades within 60 seconds. Beat 2 is the lid coming off with a visible plume of steam — that's the appetite hero. If the container has cooled between takes, the steam won't register on camera and Beat 2 falls flat. Always reheat 30 seconds between takes.
Why is the X-Rite colour checker frame shot first and not at the end?
Reveal answer
Because if the lights change between takes (brightness drift, colour drift), the reference frame needs to represent the same light conditions as the hero takes. Shooting it first and then pulling it out means the reference frame and the first hero take are captured under identical light. In post, the editor samples the grey square, syncs that WB to all subsequent frames, and the whole shoot reads as one cohesive set.
